10 Essential Rafting Tips From Our River Guides

If a rafting trip sounds like it would float your boat, but you have limited experience with the thrills and spills of the river, follow these simple white water rafting tips and your first trip will be a smooth one. Our Franklin River rafting guides and river experts have given us some essential pointers on what to wear, bring and expect on your next rafting trip.

1. Wear the right attire

While you might have every intention to stay in the boat the whole trip, you may not necessarily stay dry. Expect to get soaked on your trip, and wear waterproof and anti-chafing clothes that will act as a barrier between you and the raft. Avoid cotton, which takes a long time to dry and pulls the heat away from the body. Rather, synthetic materials are fantastic as they are quick drying. For shoes, err on the side of shoes such as enclosed shoes that have laces, such as Dunlop volleys. Thongs or flip-flops can easily be lost if you fall into the water.

Depending on the predicted weather and temperatures, you may have the option of wearing wetsuits. These will give you added buoyancy and also protect you from the cooler temperatures of the water and colder months.

2. Wear your helmet

Your safety is paramount on all of our rafting trips, which means that you always have to wear protective equipment, including helmets. There are many submerged rocks, logs and branches that aren’t visible from your sitting position in the boat, and hitting your head against these if you fall in is an experience we want to avoid. Keep your helmet on at all times and minimise your risk of injury.

3. Don’t take your valuables

The general rule of thumb for rafting trips is that you don’t bring anything that you don’t want to get lost or wet. Leave your jewellery, including rings, necklaces, earrings and bracelets at home.

When preparing for a rafting trip, it's important to be mindful of the items you bring along, as the environment can be quite challenging. The general rule of thumb is simple: avoid bringing anything that you cannot afford to lose or that would be ruined if it gets wet. Rafting adventures often involve turbulent waters and unpredictable moments, and even well-secured belongings can end up misplaced or damaged.

Jewelry, such as rings, necklaces, earrings, and bracelets, is particularly vulnerable. Not only could these items slip off unnoticed during the activity, but they may also get snagged or tangled, posing a safety risk. Additionally, exposure to water can tarnish certain types of jewelry, making it best to leave them safely at home. It's far better to enjoy the experience without the worry of protecting valuable or sentimental items. This approach allows you to focus on the thrill and beauty of the adventure rather than being concerned about safeguarding your belongings. For a smooth and stress-free experience, it's a good idea to pack light and prioritize practicality when deciding what to bring. 4. Ensure you are a competent swimmer

While you don’t have to be an Olympic swimmer to join a rafting trip, you need to be comfortable in the water. Our life jackets or personal floatation devices will keep your head above water, helping you actively swim to avoid obstacles and swim towards the boat or shore while on the river.

5. Keep your lifejacket on and tight

Just as wearing your helmet is non-negotiable during a rafting trip, keeping your life jacket securely fastened at all times is equally vital—whether you're navigating rough rapids or gliding through calm waters. Life jackets, or personal flotation devices (PFDs), do more than simply keep you afloat; they are a critical safety measure that ensures you're protected in case of unexpected falls into the water.

Beyond their buoyancy, PFDs are designed with practical features that aid in rescue situations. If you fall out of the raft, the straps and design of the life jacket make it easier for guides to grab hold of you and pull you back into the raft efficiently. This not only saves precious time during an emergency but also minimizes the effort needed to bring you back to safety.

Even in calm waters, it's best to stay prepared for any sudden shifts in conditions. By wearing your life jacket continuously and keeping it securely fastened, you create an extra layer of protection that helps you and your rafting companions stay safe throughout the journey. 6. Know what to do if you fall out of the raft

Try as we might, sometimes our guides simply can’t prevent you from falling out of the raft. If and when this happens, it’s important to know what to do next. Our guides will go through this with you in more detail at the beginning of the trip, but in preparation, remember:

  • Swim towards the raft and grab onto the outside safety line. Call out to your rafting mates in the raft and wait for someone to pull you back into the raft. Keep your life vest on, as they will use this to grab onto to hoist you into the boat.
  • If you can’t make it to the outside safety line, call out to your rafting mates to toss you the throw bag (rescue bag with a rope attached) or pass you a paddle to hold on to.
  • If you do not manage to hold onto either of these within a few seconds, assume the whitewater swimming position. Lie on your back with your feet pointing downstream. Our rafting guides will reach you.

7. Understand the procedure if someone else falls out of the raft

Our guides are highly skilled in maintaining safety during rafting trips and are ready to assist anyone who unexpectedly ends up in the water. However, if you’re in the raft and witness someone fall out, there are a couple of simple actions you can take to help:

  1. If they’re holding the outside safety line:

    Grab their life jacket by the shoulder straps and firmly pull them back into the raft. Work together with others in the raft to make this process smoother.

  2. If they’re unable to grab the safety line:
    • Use your paddle to extend it toward them so they can hold on, and gently pull them closer to the raft.
    • Toss them the rescue throw bag, a rope designed specifically for such situations. Once they grab onto it, pull them steadily back to the raft.

8. Master the T-grip

The reason the T-grip is so important is that it prevents the paddle from slipping out of your hands and hitting yourself – or your rafting mates – in the head and chest. The T-grip is the end part of the paddle that looks like a “T” and is made of hard plastic. Your guides will demonstrate the T-Grip at the beginning of your rafting trip, however essentially, one hand should always be at the base of the paddle on the shaft, and the other should always be over the T-grip. This helps ensure you maintain control of the paddle, as well as cushion any blows to yourself or your rafting mates if something should happen.

Impoprtance of T-grip

Importance of the T-Grip in Rafting

Prevents Slips: Holding onto the T-grip firmly ensures that the paddle stays securely in your hands, even during challenging maneuvers. This helps prevent accidents caused by a dropped paddle in turbulent waters.

Avoids Injuries: The T-grip minimizes the risk of accidentally hitting yourself or your rafting companions with the paddle. Proper handling keeps everyone safe from head or chest injuries.

Ensures Control: Keeping one hand on the T-grip and the other on the paddle shaft allows for precise control of your strokes, making it easier to navigate rapids and steer the raft effectively.

Promotes Teamwork: Mastering the T-grip contributes to synchronized paddling, enhancing the overall coordination and efficiency of the team during the trip.

9. Paddle! Paddle harder!

Although the river's current plays a significant role in propelling the raft forward, it alone cannot ensure a safe and effective passage through the rapids. The real work lies in the coordinated efforts of the paddlers aboard the raft. Every paddle stroke contributes to guiding the raft with precision, helping to navigate around obstacles like submerged boulders, rocks, or sudden changes in the flow of water.

When our guides call out “paddle,” it’s not just a casual suggestion; it’s an essential command that demands immediate action. Their guidance ensures that the team works together in harmony, allowing the raft to maneuver through challenging sections of the river safely. Proper paddling isn’t just about effort but about timing, technique, and teamwork.

Certain maneuvers require an entire crew to work in unison to steer clear of hazards and maintain balance. This means paying close attention to the strokes called out by your guide, who will communicate directions to help synchronize your movements. Following these instructions not only enhances your rafting experience but also ensures the safety and stability of the raft and its occupants.

10. Listen to your guide

Our guides are highly skilled professionals with extensive wilderness and rafting expertise, having spent years navigating rivers and perfecting their craft. Whether you're a first-timer or a seasoned rafter, it is essential to pay close attention to their commands. Their instructions are not only based on their wealth of experience but also tailored to ensure everyone’s safety and enjoyment throughout the trip.

While on the river, our guides will cover everything you need to know to have a successful adventure. From advising you on proper attire, explaining where it’s safe to swim, highlighting hazards to avoid, and coordinating paddling efforts, they ensure you’re well-prepared for every situation. Their directions are simple and easy to follow, with commands like “lean left,” “lean right,” “paddle,” or “stop paddling” designed to dictate the raft's direction, speed, and weight distribution effectively. These instructions are vital for navigating challenging rapids and maintaining stability.

Even if you’ve been whitewater rafting before, it’s important to recognize that our guides have more experience on the river than most participants. They’ve encountered all sorts of scenarios, from routine challenges to unpredictable obstacles, and their primary responsibility is to safeguard everyone on the trip. Trusting their expertise not only enhances your experience but also ensures the group can tackle the river as a unified team.

At times, participants may need to assist in portages—situations where rafts need to be unloaded and carried around impassable rapids. This is a temporary but essential task to help the group continue its journey smoothly and safely. Above all, remember that rafting is a group activity, and everyone’s contribution is valued. Whether it’s responding promptly to guide commands, helping during portages, or paddling in sync with others, active participation from all members strengthens the team dynamic and makes the adventure even more rewarding.
